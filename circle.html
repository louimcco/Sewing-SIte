What is a Circle Skirt?

A circle skirt is a great choice for creating a garment with lots of movement and volume whilst still retaining an air of elegence. Due to the structure of the skirt being circular, minimal fabric is found towards the top of the piece and plenty of fabric towards the bottom. This creates a smooth top and a gorgeous flowy base, suitable for mini, midi, and maxi styles.

Instructions:

To use the calculator, first begin by taking waist and length measurements. For a standalone skirt, you may want to add a little give to your waist measurement so that it doesn't fit too tight to the skin. For a gown, ensure that the waist measurement matches that of the lower hem of the dress bodice so that they may be attached without any issues.

Once you have your measurements, you then need to decide what fullness of skirt you are looking for, whether it is to be gathered, and how many panels you wish to use for construction. All of these options are discussed in detail later on in this page.

After adding your desired seam allowance and hemline, click the submit button and you will be presented with three results: the waist radius - the radius from the centre to the waistline of the skirt, the skirt radius - the radius from the centre to the end of the skirt, and the central angle of each segment for the number of panels which you have entered.

Skirt Types:

Full Circle: This is your classic circle skirt creating a super full, flowing piece which is displayed to its full potential with added layers or a petticoat underneath.

Three Quarter Circle: As the name suggests, this skirt is made from fabric cut to resemble three-quarters of a circle. This still creates a fairly flowy finished product, but without requiring quite so much fabric.

Half Circle: A half circle skirt forms a cemi-circle when laid flat, and is a great choice for an easy-to-make, simple-looking skirt. There isn't quite so much volume as with the previous two skirts so is ideal for more day-to-day wear.

Quarter Circle: Finally, a quarter skirt is formed of a 90Â° circle segment and (depending on skirt length) can create different looks. If made as mini, you will have a classic pencil-skirt type piece. Anything longer than the knee and you will start to get more flow until reaching maxi length, creating a skirt with a more fitted hip area and some extra movement towards the ankles.

Gathered Skirts:

A great way to add even more flow and volume is to have your skirt gathered at the waist. You need to increase your waist measurement to match the level of gather that your looking for (twice the waist size for lots of gathers). This method is a great option if you want an elasticated waist or you want your skirt to sit more full on the hips. It's also a great option for if you're making a skirt to go over a large petticoat (potentially for a ballgown) and you need the extra fabric.

Number of Panels:

One of the calculator options is the number of skirt panels. That is to say, how many pieces are used for construction. Generally, two panels will suffice to make a garment that is simple with some basic structure. By using more panels, you can create a more interesting final piece or (if your limited on fabric) you can make the most of your yardage with some strategic fabric cutting.
